Q1: Nigeria Crop Production Trends 2000–2023
========================================================================
Cassava remains Nigeria's dominant crop by volume, growing from 32.0
million tonnes in 2000 to 62.0 million tonnes in 2023 — underpinning
food security for over 200 million people. Oil palm fruit production
expanded by 54% over the same period, reflecting growing commercial
interest in the palm belt. Maize and sorghum show moderate, consistent
growth tied to both food and livestock feed demand. Cocoa production
remains constrained by aging tree stock and limited replanting
investment in the South West.


========================================================================
Q2: Highest-Yield Zone per Crop
========================================================================
Yield performance is strongly zone-differentiated. Top zones by crop:
Cassava: South West; Cocoa beans: South West; Maize: South West; Oil
palm fruit: North Central; Rice (paddy): South West; Sorghum: South
West; Yam: South West. The South South and South East lead for Oil palm
and Cassava — consistent with their high-rainfall, high-humidity climate
profiles. Northern zones dominate for drought-tolerant crops: North West
and North Central lead for Sorghum and Maize respectively, leveraging
longer dry seasons and lower humidity that these cereals prefer. These
structural advantages should anchor zone-specific input-planning
strategies.

Q3: Nigeria vs Global Yield Benchmarks
========================================================================
Nigeria's most striking yield deficit is in Oil Palm — the crop where it
holds the most commercial potential. Nigeria averages approximately 2.9
t/ha, while the global average is 17.0 t/ha and Malaysia reaches 24.5
t/ha. This 83% shortfall against world average — widening to over 50%
against Malaysian best practice — represents the single largest
unrealised agricultural value opportunity in the Nigerian economy.
Fertilizer application in Nigeria's palm-growing south averages 40–60
kg/ha against best-practice plantations using 150–200 kg/ha. Cassava
shows a similar pattern: Nigeria produces well below Thailand's
benchmark despite holding the world's largest planted cassava area.

Q4: Rainfall–Cassava Yield Relationship
========================================================================
The correlation between annual rainfall and cassava yield is weak and
positive (Pearson r = 0.01). South South and South East zones —
receiving 1,800–2,400 mm annually — consistently achieve higher cassava
yields than northern zones receiving 500–700 mm. However, the
relationship is non-linear: above approximately 2,000 mm/year,
waterlogging effects suppress yields, explaining why the wettest South
South micro-plots can underperform drier South East plots. This finding
supports zone-differentiated planting calendars that align cassava
planting with the rainfall onset rather than peak wet season.

Q5: Fertilizer Use Intensity by Zone
========================================================================
North West leads all zones with an average application of 165.1 kg/ha of
fertilizer (N+P+K combined), reflecting commercial maize and sorghum
farming systems with higher input adoption. North West shows the lowest
intensity at 165.1 kg/ha — far below agronomic recommendations for the
high-value palm and cocoa crops grown there. Across all zones, the N:P:K
ratio is heavily nitrogen-skewed, consistent with subsistence-oriented
urea application rather than balanced compound fertilizer programmes.
Rebalancing toward phosphate and potash is the first lever for yield
improvement in the southern zones.

Q6: Crop Yield Gap Analysis
========================================================================
Every crop shows a material gap between the national average and the
performance achieved by leading zones. Yam carries the largest relative
gap at 18% below its in-country 90th-percentile benchmark. Critically,
these gaps are not theoretical — they represent yields already achieved
somewhere in Nigeria on Nigerian soil, with Nigerian farmers. The
performance ceiling is domestically proven; the task is scaling best-
practice inputs, timing, and agronomic knowledge from leading zones to
lagging zones.

Q7: Crop Area Expansion vs Contraction
========================================================================
Expanding crops (area up >5% vs early 2000s): Cassava, Oil palm fruit,
Yam, Maize, Rice (paddy), Sorghum, Cocoa beans. Contracting crops: None.
Cassava area expansion is the most prominent trend — driven by urban
demand for garri, fufu, and starch processing. Oil palm area is growing
in line with Nigeria's domestic consumption deficit (imports were needed
every year 2000–2023). Where cocoa area is declining, tree aging and
limited replanting investment are the primary drivers — representing a
long-run supply risk for Nigeria's cocoa export revenue.

Q8: Best Five-Year Maize Production Windows
========================================================================
The highest 5-year cumulative maize production period in Nigeria was
2019–2023, generating approximately 57.6 million tonnes. This window
coincides with increased public investment in the Growth Enhancement
Scheme (GES) and e-wallet fertilizer subsidy programmes, which improved
input access for smallholder maize farmers in the North Central and
North West zones. The insight reinforces that structured input subsidy
programmes have a measurable and sustained multi-year impact on maize
output — not merely a single-season spike.

Q9: Fertilizer-to-Yield Efficiency Trend
========================================================================
The fertilizer efficiency ratio (tonnes of crop per tonne of fertilizer
applied) has declined from 425.0 in 2000 to 380.9 in 2023. A declining
ratio signals that fertilizer application is growing faster than output
— a warning sign of either input waste, poor timing, or application on
crops and soils with low marginal response. An improving ratio indicates
that yield gains are outpacing input cost growth — the direction any
agri-business programme should target. Zone-level efficiency analysis
(Module 4 input optimiser) will identify which specific crop-zone
combinations are driving this aggregate trend.

Q10: Year-on-Year Yield Volatility by Zone
========================================================================
Yield risk — measured as the coefficient of variation (CV) of annual
yield across 2000–2023 — varies materially across zones. North West zone
shows the highest average yield CV, indicating the most unpredictable
production environment for farmers and input planners alike. North West
zone achieves the most stable year-on-year performance. High CV zones
present both a planning challenge and an opportunity: their wide yield
variance means that targeted agronomic interventions (irrigation,
drought-tolerant varieties, timely fertilizer) have the potential to
generate outsized productivity gains by reducing variance rather than
only lifting the mean.

Q11: Seasonal Rainfall Pattern Across Zones
========================================================================
The heat map confirms Nigeria's two distinct agronomic rainfall regimes.
Northern zones (North West, North East) experience a sharply unimodal
pattern: virtually all rainfall arrives between June and September, with
an absolute dry season from November through March. Southern zones
(South West, South East, South South) show a bimodal pattern with wet
peaks in May–June and again in September–October — allowing two planting
seasons per year for short-duration crops like maize and early cassava.
North Central occupies a transitional position. Planting calendar
recommendations (Module 5) are built directly on these seasonal
profiles.

Q12: Zone-Crop Improvement Opportunity Matrix
========================================================================
The highest-priority zone-crop combination — defined by largest yield
gap multiplied by largest planted area — is North Central / Yam
(opportunity score: 100/100). This combination offers the maximum return
on agronomic investment: large land area means even a modest yield
improvement translates into millions of tonnes of additional output. The
top-right quadrant of the matrix (high gap, high area) is where targeted
interventions — improved seed varieties, balanced fertilizer, and timed
planting — will deliver the highest system-level impact for Nigeria's
food security and agricultural GDP growth.
